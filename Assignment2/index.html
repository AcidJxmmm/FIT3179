Wildfires are becoming a greater concern across the United States as a result of climate change, forest management, and an expanding wildland-urban interface.

Trends in wildfire activity, expenses, and destruction between 1992 and 2010 serve as an example of how the effects of wildfires are increasing. Over the duration of 18 years, it can be clearly seen that the wildfires do not only increase in number but also in size.

As to size of wildfire:

  • Class A - 1/4 acre or less.
  • Class B - more than 1/4 acre, but less than 10 acres.
  • Class C - 10 acres or more, but less than 100 acres.
  • Class D - 100 acres or more, but less than 300 acres.
  • Class E - 300 acres or more, but less than 1,000 acres.
  • Class F - 1,000 acres or more, but less than 5,000.
  • Class G - 5,000 acres or more.

Since lightning began to light up its sky, Florida has experienced fires on a cycle of every three to five years. Due to seasonal dry spells and lightning-sparked flames, Florida's environment encourages fires. Historically, fires have only burned in Florida landscapes every three to seven years. The abundant rainfall and extended growing seasons that encourage quick plant growth make these brief cycles feasible. Ecosystems would burn easily and swiftly recover.

Every year, fires that can break out at any time of the year due to a number of reasons, including as arson, lightning, and debris burning, damage thousands of acres of wilderness and several residences. The state's wildland firefighters are under even more strain as a result of this increase. Due to this expansion, everyone is now responsible for fire prevention.
